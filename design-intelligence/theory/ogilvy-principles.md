# Ogilvy Principles — Applied to Sales Pages & Landing Pages

> **Source:** David Ogilvy, *Ogilvy on Advertising* (1983) + *Ogilvy on Advertising in the Digital Age* (2018). Adapted for web/LP context.
> **Purpose:** Every editorial-style sales page in this system traces its copy and layout decisions to these principles.

---

## The Ogilvy Layout Formula

Ogilvy discovered through rigorous testing that advertisements following this layout order outperform all others. Adapted for web:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                             │
│          VISUAL (3/4 of viewport)           │
│    Large, arresting, editorial-quality      │
│                                             │
│─────────────────────────────────────────────│
│  Caption under image (most-read text        │
│  after headline — 2x more readers than      │
│  body copy)                                 │
│─────────────────────────────────────────────│
│                                             │
│  HEADLINE                                   │
│  6-12 words, benefit-driven                 │
│                                             │
│─────────────────────────────────────────────│
│                                             │
│  BODY COPY                                  │
│  Long-form editorial, serif type            │
│  Column width ≤ 40 characters (print)       │
│  or ≤ 75ch (web)                            │
│                                             │
│─────────────────────────────────────────────│
│                                             │
│  CTA — Single, clear action                 │
│                                             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Web adaptation notes:**
- The "3/4 visual" translates to a hero section occupying at least 75vh
- Caption becomes a subtle text overlay or the first line below the hero image
- On mobile, the visual may compress to 50vh but should remain dominant

## The 10 Ogilvy Headline Rules

Ogilvy said: *"On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar."*

### Rules for Sales Page Headlines

1. **Include the benefit.** The headline must promise the reader a reward for reading.
2. **6-12 words.** Headlines of 6-12 words pull more coupon responses than shorter or longer ones.
3. **Include news.** Headlines with "new", "introducing", "announcing" recall 22% higher.
4. **Include numbers.** Specificity signals credibility. "How I improved my memory in one evening" outperforms "How to improve your memory".
5. **Localize when possible.** "How dentists in São Paulo are..." outpulls generic equivalents.
6. **Promise the reader useful information.** "How-to" headlines consistently outperform clever or abstract ones.
7. **Never use a headline you'd be ashamed to use for your own family.** No tricks, no bait-and-switch.
8. **Put the brand name in the headline** if you're building brand awareness. Otherwise, lead with the benefit.
9. **Avoid blind headlines** — those that mean nothing unless you read the body copy.
10. **Test headlines.** Ogilvy's research showed that changing a headline alone could change response rates by a factor of 10.

### Headline Patterns That Consistently Win

| Pattern | Example | Why It Works |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| How-to | "How to ship 3x faster without hiring" | Promises useful information |
| News | "Introducing the first AI that..." | Novelty triggers attention |
| Question | "Do you make these mistakes in...?" | Self-referencing engages |
| Command | "Stop wasting 15 hours a week on..." | Direct, urgent |
| Testimonial | "This tool cut our costs 40% — Sarah, VP Marketing" | Third-party credibility |
| Number | "7 ways to double your conversion rate" | Specificity + finite commitment |

## Long Copy vs. Short Copy

Ogilvy's most controversial principle: *"All my experience says that for a great many products, long copy sells more than short."*

### When Long Copy Works (use for sales pages)

- **High-consideration products:** Anything over $100 or requiring a commitment
- **Complex products:** SaaS, courses, consulting where the buyer needs to understand what they get
- **Problem-aware audiences:** People who know they have a problem and need convincing that YOUR solution is the right one
- **First-time visitors:** People who've never heard of you need more information, not less
- **Information-seeking audiences:** B2B, technical products, education

### When Short Copy Works

- **Low-consideration products:** Under $50, impulse-friendly
- **Most-aware audiences:** They already know and trust you — just show the offer
- **Repeat purchases:** Returning customers need less convincing
- **Visual products:** Fashion, food, travel where images sell more than words
- **Time-sensitive offers:** Flash sales, limited-time deals

### The Ogilvy Long-Copy Rules

1. **Every sentence must earn its place.** Long does not mean padded. Cut every sentence that doesn't advance the argument.
2. **Use subheadings.** Break long copy into scannable sections. Readers who won't read every word will still scan subheadings.
3. **Drop caps and bold leads.** Start key paragraphs with a visual signal. The first word matters.
4. **No widow lines.** Dense paragraphs of 11+ lines lose readers. Keep paragraphs to 3-5 lines.
5. **Serif fonts for long editorial.** Ogilvy used serif exclusively. On web, serif body copy at 18px+ with 1.6 line-height achieves the same readability.

## Before/After Format

Ogilvy considered before/after the single most persuasive advertising technique:

*"Before-and-after advertisements are above average in their ability to attract attention and communicate."*

### Application to Sales Pages

```
┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
│     BEFORE      │     AFTER       │
│                 │                 │
│ - Problem state │ - Desired state │
│ - Pain metrics  │ - Result metrics│
│ - Screenshot    │ - Screenshot    │
│ - Testimonial   │ - Testimonial   │
│   (pain)        │   (joy)         │
│                 │                 │
│ "15 hours/week  │ "2 hours/week   │
│  on reporting"  │  on reporting"  │
└─────────────────┴─────────────────┘
```

### Rules for Before/After

1. **Specificity.** Vague before/after is useless. Use metrics, screenshots, real data.
2. **Same person.** The before and after must clearly be the same person/company/situation.
3. **Credible gap.** The improvement must be believable. "0 to $1M in a week" destroys trust.
4. **Visual proof.** Screenshots, charts, photos beat text-only before/after.

## Editorial-Style Layouts

Ogilvy proved that advertisements designed to look like editorial content outperform conventional ad layouts. On the web, this translates to:

### Editorial LP Characteristics

| Element | Convention | Editorial (Ogilvy) |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| Layout | Grid-based, symmetric | Asymmetric, magazine-like |
| Typography | Sans-serif, uniform | Serif body, contrast display |
| Headline size | 32-48px | 56-80px, bold statement |
| Image treatment | Rounded, shadows | Full-bleed, no frames |
| Body copy | Bullet points, cards | Flowing paragraphs, columns |
| CTA style | Bold button, high-contrast | Subtle text link or understated button |
| White space | Minimal, packed | Generous, breathing |
| Color palette | Brand colors everywhere | Restrained, 2-3 colors max |

### When to Use Editorial Style

- Premium positioning (luxury, authority brands)
- Products that sell through credibility and depth
- Audiences that read (professionals, B2B)
- Ogilvy Direct framework (see narrative skill)

### When NOT to Use

- Mass-market, impulse products
- Audiences that scan more than read
- Mobile-first pages where editorial density overwhelms small screens

## Research-First Approach

*"Advertising people who ignore research are as dangerous as generals who ignore decodes of enemy signals."*

### Application to the Pipeline

| Phase | Research Activity |
|-------|------------------|
| Phase 1 (Discovery) | Product research — understand what you're selling |
| Phase 2 (Research) | Competitor research — what they do well and poorly |
| Phase 2 (Research) | Audience research — their language, fears, desires |
| Phase 5 (Narrative) | Copy testing — headline scoring with 4U formula |
| Phase 8 (QA) | Performance research — does it actually convert? |

### What Ogilvy Would Research Today

1. **Competitor landing pages** — what promises they make, what proof they show
2. **Audience language** — Reddit threads, support tickets, reviews (their words, not yours)
3. **Search queries** — what they Google when they have this problem
4. **Heatmaps** — where people click, how far they scroll (for iteration)

## "If It Doesn't Sell, It Isn't Creative"

The most important Ogilvy principle for sales pages:

*"When I write an advertisement, I don't want you to tell me that you find it 'creative.' I want you to find it so interesting that you buy the product."*

### How This Applies

1. **No decorative animations.** Every animation must serve a purpose (reveal content, guide attention, demonstrate product).
2. **No clever-for-clever's-sake headlines.** If the reader doesn't understand the promise in 3 seconds, rewrite.
3. **No stock photo backgrounds.** Use product screenshots, real photos, or high-quality abstract visuals that reinforce the message.
4. **No font experimentation for its own sake.** If the font makes reading harder, it failed.
5. **Measure everything.** A beautiful page that converts at 0.5% is worse than an ugly page that converts at 3%.

## Single Objective Principle

*"A good advertisement is one which sells the product without drawing attention to itself."*

### Application

Every sales page has ONE primary objective. One action you want the visitor to take.

| Objective | CTA | Everything Else |
|-----------|-----|----------------|
| Buy | "Buy Now" / "Enroll" | Supports the buying decision |
| Sign Up | "Start Free Trial" | Reduces friction to signing up |
| Book | "Schedule a Call" | Builds confidence to commit time |
| Download | "Get the Guide" | Creates enough value to exchange email |

**The rule:** If a section doesn't support the primary objective, cut it. Secondary CTAs are permitted but must not compete with or distract from the primary.

## Brand Consistency

*"You cannot bore people into buying your product; you can only interest them in buying it."*

Ogilvy maintained consistent brand voices for decades (Rolls-Royce, Dove, Schweppes). For sales pages:

1. **Same voice across all pages.** If your brand is casual, every sales page is casual. No switching to corporate.
2. **Same visual language.** Colors, fonts, spacing should be recognizable across pages.
3. **Same proof standards.** If one page uses real metrics, don't fall back to vague claims on another.
4. **Same ethical standards.** If your brand doesn't use fake urgency, no page gets an exception.

## Quick Reference: Ogilvy Decision Matrix

| Situation | Ogilvy Would Say |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Headline isn't working | "Include the benefit in the first 6 words" |
| Page feels too long | "Long copy sells — but cut every word that doesn't advance the argument" |
| Design is beautiful but doesn't convert | "If it doesn't sell, it isn't creative" |
| Client wants to be clever | "You aren't writing to impress. You're writing to sell." |
| Multiple CTAs competing | "One objective per advertisement" |
| No visual proof | "Before/after is the most persuasive format" |
| Using stock photos | "Use photographs of real people, not models" |
| Body copy uses sans-serif | "Set body copy in serif for serious editorial" |
